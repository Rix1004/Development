The Story

Harvard Student (HS):

“So, yeah, I mean, once you understand the DOM and how to manipulate it with JavaScript
everything else is just layering functionality.
Of course, that’s if you’ve already mastered semantic HTML and responsive CSS,
which, you know, can take years of dedicated study.”

Will:

“Or, you could just learn it all in a few months if you’re not wasting your time sounding
like an overpriced Udemy course.”

HS:

“Oh, really? And you think you know better? Why don’t you enlighten us?”

Will:

“Sure. What you just said about ‘mastering semantic HTML’?
That’s from HTML & CSS: Design and Build Websites by Jon Duckett. Chapter 2, right?
You probably skimmed it last night so you could sprinkle the word ‘semantic' into a conversation and sound informed.
Then you tacked on JavaScript because you saw a thread on Reddit where someone mentioned how
‘you can’t just write HTML and CSS anymore, bro.’ Am I getting warm?”

HS:

“Well, uh—”

Will (cutting him off):

“And as for ‘responsive CSS,’ you’re probably parroting some Bootstrap tutorial on YouTube.
Let me guess—grid system blew your mind, huh? But you still can’t write a media query without
copying and pasting one from Stack Overflow.”

HS (defensive):

“Hey, look, man—”

Will (leaning in):

“No, see, the problem here isn’t learning to code.
It’s that you’re too busy dropping buzzwords to impress people instead of writing actual code.
You think reading the first few chapters of a textbook makes you a developer?
Here’s the thing: you could’ve learned everything you just said for free by opening Mozilla’s MDN docs or doing the tutorials on freeCodeCamp.
But no, you’re too busy paying $10,000 for a bootcamp so they can give you a certificate no one will ever ask for.”
